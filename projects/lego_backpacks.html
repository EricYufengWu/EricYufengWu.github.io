LEGO SPIKE Prime Backpacks

June 2019 - August 2019

As a hardware summer intern at Tufts University Center for Engineering education and outreach, I made a series of LEGO "backpacks" - custom-made housings that connect the new LEGO SPIKE Prime engineering education kit to other non-LEGO, 3rd party sensors and microcontrollers. The main goal of making the backpacks is to help LEGO explore the posibility of incorporating more advanced sensors into their educational products. After I finished making the backpacks, there were handed to the testing team who will test the backpacks and develop demonstrations to present to LEGO.

This project helped me learn how to design under constraints, as well as how to combine differnet hardware skills to solve engineering problems. In this page I will show you how I developed this product from sketching on an iPad to the final version.

First, a closer look:

The backpacks I designed pressure-fit into the existing LEGO SPIKE Prime sensor ports - that is, both the physical structure and the pin headers inside. To achieve that, each 3D printed shell is combined with a printed circuit board to mimic the way the real LEGO sensors connect to their housing. In this way, I can design different circuits on the other side for 3rd party sensors. As long as I keep the connection side consistent, the backpacks will snap into place and work well with the LEGO system.

This universal mounting system was later used for other interns across the center to design more backpacks.

Design Process

From sketching to prototyping

The first backpack I design was for the OpenMV Camera. My very first step is to try to sketch out my ideas on ipad. In the sketch, I sketched out the contours of the connection point, and oriented the camera to a desirable position. After the initial sketch I realized that I need a mini PCB to convert the pin dimension on the OpenMV to the LEGO port. With that idea in mind, I started CADing the case and the PCB.

Sketching and CADing

PCB design version 1

I worked back-and-forth between my 3D models and my printed circuit boards. I made test PCBs from the Othermill machine while printing out prototype cases on a Form 2 printer, and from time to time I had to change one to better fit the other. As the number of prototypes I made increases, I started putting in more features, such as making holes for USB port, jumper wires, and LEGO connectors. Finally, I designed a snap-in lid for the case, which took me a few more rounds of prototyping to get the dimensions right.

After a lot of iterations I ended up with the following result: A "backpack" that consists of a LEGO-compatible case, a snap-in lid, and a built-in pcb that connects to the OpenMV camera without extra bolts and nuts.
